I don’t think anyone can truly predict the future of user interface paradigms. Fifteen years ago, some might have confidently claimed that mobile would replace every other platform. Mobile is hugely important; however, there are still many successful companies that largely ignore it. Others argued that Google Calendar and Outlook would render paper calendars obsolete, yet every Christmas I still receive a Beaches of Hawaii wall-hanging calendar from my in-laws.

Now, I hear predictions that, within a few years, all of our computer interactions will take place through an agentic interface. Will this time be any different?

## Why this prediction might come true

Agentic interfaces are getting a lot more useful. With Generative UI, agents now have the ability to dynamically generate just about any interface element on demand. That means agents will be able to not only fetch and visualize information, but also create inputs users can use to interact with to accomplish complex tasks, all without the need for a designer to hand-craft every possible screen or workflow.

## Problems with Agentic Interfaces

There’s no doubt agentic interfaces will play a big role in the future of software. But if we’re talking about what makes a tool easy to use, these interfaces come up short in some important ways.

### 1\. Lack of Visibility

When I say “well-designed,” I really mean “easy to use.” Folks who are bullish on AI often see natural language interfaces as simple because they hide all the system’s complexity behind a chat with an agent. But here’s where things start to get tricky. Sometimes, agentic interfaces hide _too much_, making it hard for users to figure out what the system actually does.

A good tool doesn’t just work, it lets you see how it works. It gives you feedback about what’s happening and drops hints about how to use it, all without a manual. Think about doors: a push plate means “push,” a handle means “pull.” If there’s nothing there, you’re left standing around, wondering what to do next.

Let’s take elevators as another example. Imagine stepping into a voice-operated elevator. You say your floor, and it just takes you there. But you look around for those familiar buttons, and there’s nothing... just blank walls. Suddenly, you’re left with questions: How many floors does this building have? Which floor am I on now? Is this even an elevator? Sure, after some trial and error with the voice agent, you’ll figure it out. But you’ll probably miss having those buttons—not just the physical feel, but the instant feedback and control they offer.

In short, agentic interfaces can make things harder by hiding too much. When users can’t see what’s going on or what their options are, the interface ends up being more confusing than helpful.

### 2\. Inconsistency and Recall

Imagine you visit an app for your local zoo. It has data on all the animals who live there. When you last used the app you saw a really neat interactive graph of elephant births over time. Maybe you want to show it to your kid this time. "How did I get there again?" You ask yourself. Usually you would look for clues: familiar phrases, highlighted links, accordions that remain open, paths to pages remembered by your browser.

In the agentic UI, these clues are missing. Your only option is to ask the agent. And unfortunately, LLMs are non-deterministic. It might assemble you something entirely new this time out of the lego blocks it's developers have given it to answer your questions. You're basically rolling the dice on what kind of experience you'll get, every time you use the app.

### 3\. Unconventional

How does anyone know how to use anything? By looking at it and determining what they know about it from past experiences. Currently, agentic interfaces are new. So by nature they are unconventional. Some predict this will change as AI adoption becomes more widespread. I am skeptical as I consider the many design problems these kinds of interfaces have.

My advice: don't rush to an agentic interface for your application unless your user research and testing indicates its the best way to solve your users' problems. Prepare for change and learn how these tools can benefit your application, but don't fall victim to a fad. If and when it does become the default convention users expect, then you should certainly adopt it.
